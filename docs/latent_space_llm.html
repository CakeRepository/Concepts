What is Latent Space?

An LLM does not understand characters directly. It translates every token (word or word fragment) into a high-dimensional vector in a coordinate space called the Latent Space.

In this high-dimensional space (often 4,096 dimensions or more), geometric closeness corresponds to semantic similarity. Words with similar functional or conceptual meaning end up sitting next to each other.

\(\vec{v}_{\text{cat}} \approx [0.45, -0.12, \dots, 0.89]\)

For visualization purposes, we project this multi-thousand-dimensional coordinate system down to 3D. The mountains and basins show the likelihood of different paths in the landscape depending on your input context.

// Visualizing Coordinate Projections
Context: "Hey, how's it going?"
Vector State: \(\vec{E}_{\text{context}}\) = center node [0, 0, 40]

Valley 2 (Casual): "Not" (Coord: [-35, 35, -5])
Valley 3 (Brief): "Busy" (Coord: [-35, -35, -2])

Navigating the Loss Surface

During LLM training, we use Gradient Descent to shape this terrain. By calculating gradients (slopes of error), weights in the network are updated so that the "loss" (or likelihood error) drops to a minimum.

During inference (text generation), we can think of the model's current sequence history as a ball positioned on the landscape. The steepness of the slopes represents probability gradients: steeper drops lead to more likely/probable tokens.

\(\vec{F}_{\text{gradient}} = -m \cdot g \cdot \nabla Z(x, y)\)

The ball rolls down the potential energy slopes, accelerating along the negative gradient vector to find a local minimum: the next word in the sentence.

// Gradient Calculations
Potential Energy Field Z(x,y)
Slope vector \(\nabla Z = [\partial Z/\partial x, \partial Z/\partial y]\)
Rolling Ball Velocity: \(\vec{V}_{t+1} = \vec{V}_{t} - \eta \nabla Z\)
Energy State = \(E_{\text{potential}}\)

Modulating Temperature ($T$)

In text generation, Temperature alters the model's confidence distribution. At a temperature of $0.0$, the model is strictly deterministic: the deepest valley is chosen every single time (greedy search).

As temperature increases, the probability peaks are flattened out, and random thermal noise (Brownian motion) is added to the ball. This represents the stochastic sampling of words.

\(P_i = \text{Softmax}(z_i / T)\)

Visually, turning up the Temperature adds random force vectors to the ball, making it wobble and jump over mountain ridges, occasionally settling in completely different, unexpected word basins!

// Stochastic Noise Simulator
At Temperature T = 1.2:
Thermal Force vector: \(\vec{F}_{\text{noise}} \propto \text{random} \times T\)
Ball position jitter: \(\delta x_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 \cdot T)\)
Warning: High entropy increases creative risk!

Pruning the Space: Top-K & Top-P

Without limits, high temperature could cause the LLM to choose nonsense words. To prevent this, we restrict choices using **Top-K** and **Top-P** sampling.

Top-K: Retains only the $K$ most probable valleys, disabling all others. On our 3D grid, the disabled basins are visually "fenced off" or filled with barrier blocks so the ball cannot enter them.

Top-P (Nucleus): Selects a dynamic set of words whose cumulative probability adds up to $P$ (e.g. $0.90$). The rest are discarded. This adapts the allowed search space based on how confident the model is.

\(\sum_{i \in V^{(p)}} P_i \ge p\)

Tokens Sorted by Prob: [ "It's" (0.85), "Not" (0.12), "Busy" (0.03) ]
Top-K = 2: Allowed: ["It's", "Not"] | Blocked: ["Busy"]
Top-P = 0.90: Cumulative sum "It's" (0.85) + "Not" (0.97).
